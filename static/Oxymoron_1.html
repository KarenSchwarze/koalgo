Oxymoron

An oxymoron, a literary device, is a pair of words that are opposites. You can use them in your creative writing to add emphasis to a point you want to make. Here is an example:

Why then, O brawling love, O loving hate...

O heavy lightness, serious vanity...

Feather of lead, bright smoke, cold fire, sick health...

-Romeo in Romeo annd Juliet by William Shakespeare, Act 1, lines 179, 181, 183

Do you see the pairs of opposites in the lines above? Each of those pairs is an oxymoron.

The character Romeo in the play Romeo and Juliet uses these opposites to describe the paradoxical nature of the kind of love he experiences. Perhaps one of the most famous oxymorons also comes from Romeo and Juliet when Juliet says that "Parting is such sweet sorrow", meaning that even though parting (saying goodbye) is sad, there is a sweet feeling in the experience as well.

Think of some of your own examples of oxymorons. Are you a "cheerful pessimist"? Or have you ever watched a TV show about zombies, who are the "living dead"?
